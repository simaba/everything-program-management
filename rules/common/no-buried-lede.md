# Rule: No Buried Lede

**Always load.**

The harness may not produce any document, brief, or reply whose first line is context rather than conclusion.

## Rule

The **lede** is the one sentence that tells the reader what this communication is and why it matters. It appears first. Not on page 2. Not in the fourth paragraph. First.

If the reader stops after line 1, they should still know:
- what this is about
- what decision, recommendation, or action is being communicated

## Application

### Documents
- Memos, briefs, charters, post-mortems: first sentence after the title is the lede.
- Decision memos: the question is the lede. Recommendation appears by end of first page.
- Post-mortems: the summary paragraph (what happened, what changed) is the lede.

### Chat replies
- The answer to the user's question appears before any reasoning about it.
- Reasoning can follow, if useful, labeled as "Why:" or "How I got there:".

### Agent handoffs
- "Here is the output:" *then* the output. Not "Below you will find..."
- Summary one-liner before any detail.

## Counter-examples the harness should reject

- "Over the past several weeks, the team has been investigating..."
- "This document outlines the approach to..."
- "Before discussing the recommendation, let's review the context..."

The repair for each is identical: move the recommendation / answer / conclusion to the first line. The context line becomes line 2 or moves to a "Why this matters" section.

## Tension with "preamble is polite"

Preamble is not polite in a professional exec context; it is expensive. A busy reader who gets a preamble paragraph concludes either (a) you don't know the answer or (b) you're wasting their time. Neither is the position you want.

## Source

- Journalism's "inverted pyramid" — most important information first.
- BLUF (Bottom Line Up Front) — US military staff writing, Army Regulation 25-50.
- Barbara Minto's Pyramid Principle, same rule under a different name.
