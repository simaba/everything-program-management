# Rule: Two Options or Explicit Ruled-Out

**Always load when decision-memo skill is active.**

A decision memo presents at least two viable options. If only one is presented, the memo must include an explicit "Ruled Out" section listing ≥ 2 alternatives that were considered and why they failed to qualify.

## Rule

A memo with one option is not a decision memo; it is a sales pitch. The harness must, before producing a decision memo:

1. Brainstorm at least 4 candidate options.
2. Present at least 2 of them as viable in the body.
3. Document at least 2 in a "Ruled Out" section with one-line reasons.

If the user truly believes only one option exists, the harness produces the memo with the explicit "Ruled Out" section, not by simply skipping the rule.

## Why

The most common failure mode of decision-memo writing is that the author has already decided and is performing option analysis for the record. This produces:

- Option A (recommended): exhaustive, persuasive
- Option B: token, easy to dismiss
- Option C: straw

The result: the decider reads, agrees, and the organization learns nothing about whether the decision was good. A year later, the post-mortem cannot distinguish "good decision, bad outcome" from "bad decision."

The two-options rule forces the author to actually consider alternatives. The "Ruled Out" rule forces them to document the search even if alternatives didn't make the cut.

## Application

The harness, before producing a decision memo:

1. Asks itself: what's the second option? If the answer is "there isn't one," ask: what would it look like to *not* do this? "Do nothing" is always an option and often a real one.
2. For one-option memos, populates "Ruled Out" with at least:
   - Status quo (do nothing) — with explicit cost
   - The next-most-obvious alternative
   - At least one option that was politically or culturally rejected
3. Does not skip the section as "not applicable."

## Counter-examples the harness should reject

- A memo with "Option A: do this" and no other section. Wrong.
- A memo with "Option A: do this. Option B: do nothing (rejected because it doesn't solve the problem)." Acceptable only if "Option B" is treated as a real option in the comparison, not a straw.
- A "Ruled Out" section containing only "Option B: hire a consultant — too expensive." Two more needed.

## Source

- Jeff Bezos, 2015 letter to shareholders — one-way vs two-way doors.
- Roger Martin, *The Opposable Mind* — integrative thinking requires holding ≥ 2 viable options.
- Kahneman, *Thinking, Fast and Slow*, on the WYSIATI fallacy (what you see is all there is) — the rule directly counters it.
